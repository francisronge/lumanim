Lumanim · Paradox 0003

A sentence
turns on itself.

“This sentence is false” does not report a fact elsewhere. It reaches back, evaluates itself, and reverses every classical truth value you try to give it.

Find the unusual ingredient

What distinguishes the Liar from an ordinary sentence that happens to be false?

Build the loop before evaluating it

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy writes the simple-falsity Liar as a sentence L which says of itself that L is false. The first visual exposes that reference: “this sentence” points to the complete sentence containing those words.

Self-reference alone is not enough. “This sentence has five words” also refers to itself and remains stable. The dangerous addition is a claim about its own falsehood.

Now try to evaluate it

Classical evaluation offers two starting points. Watch what the sentence does to each one.

FALSETRUE

Both doors return the opposite

  1. Assume true: what the sentence says must hold, so the sentence is false.
  2. Assume false: its claim that it is false is correct, so the sentence is true.

The Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy presents the same two-case derivation. Under ordinary truth principles and the demand that the sentence be either true or false, neither assignment remains stable.

Retrieve the reversal

What happens under either classical assignment?

Liar

A self-referential truth claim sends either attempted classical value to its opposite.

Keep

The Liar is not merely “a confusing sentence.” Its sharp structure is a failed fixed point: ordinary evaluation maps TRUE to FALSE and FALSE to TRUE, leaving no stable classical assignment.
