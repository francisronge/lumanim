Lumanim · Paradox 0002

One harmless step.
An impossible march.

The Sorites paradox does not ask you to spot one dramatic mistake. It asks whether the same reasonable judgment can survive being repeated.

Commit before watching

Suppose 24 grains are clearly a heap. What should happen after exactly one is removed?

The locally harmless step

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy describes heap as a vague predicate: clear cases exist at both ends, but its boundary is unclear. That makes the one-grain step feel harmless:

If n grains are a heap, then n − 1 grains are a heap.

The visual does not prove that sentence. It isolates the premise your intuition is tempted to grant.

Now preserve that judgment

Use exactly the same step again, and again, without inventing a special exception halfway through.

The global collision

The argument combines three commitments:

  1. Clear start: 24 grains count as a heap in this model.
  2. Tolerance: removing one grain cannot change that verdict.
  3. Repetition: the same inference applies at every adjacent step.

Classical step-by-step reasoning then carries heap all the way to one grain—an unacceptable endpoint. The exact numbers are only a compact visual model; the traditional argument uses enough grains to make the starting case indisputable.

Do not confuse the structures

Ship of Theseus

Two familiar criteria—continuous history and original material—point to different successors.

Sorites

One locally plausible tolerance rule, repeated without exception, destroys the distinction between clear endpoints.

Keep

The Sorites paradox is not merely “we do not know the cutoff.” Its pressure comes from wanting all three things at once: clear endpoint cases, no decisive one-grain change, and unrestricted step-by-step reasoning.

Primary source

Read the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy's “Sorites Paradox” for the argument's main forms and leading responses. The puzzle is traditionally credited to the fourth-century BCE dialectician Eubulides; see the Oxford Classical Dictionary for the historical attribution.
