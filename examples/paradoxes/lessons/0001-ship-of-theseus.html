The split

Plutarch reports the simpler dispute: Athenians replaced old timbers over time, and philosophers disagreed about whether the maintained vessel remained the same ship. The later reassembly case sharpens it: the continuous vessel and the vessel rebuilt from the originals now each inherit a different claim to one past.

That is not yet a solution. It is the precise conflict:

  • History favors the continuously maintained ship.
  • Matter favors the reassembled original planks.
  • Identity cannot straightforwardly belong to both if the two later ships are distinct.

The two identity claims

Continuity

Rule: the same object persists through one connected history of gradual repairs.

Points to: the continuously maintained ship.

Composition

Rule: the original matter determines which later object is the original.

Points to: the ship rebuilt from the old planks.

Keep

The Ship of Theseus is not just “everything changes.” Its sharp form asks what happens when continuity and original composition point to different objects.
